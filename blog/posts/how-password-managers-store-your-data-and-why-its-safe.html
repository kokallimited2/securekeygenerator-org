How Password Managers Store Your Data — And Why It's Safe

Worried about putting all your passwords in one place? Here's the cryptography behind how good password managers keep your data secure.

Advertisement

The common concern: "If I put all my passwords in one place, won't hackers just go there?" The answer reveals just how clever modern password managers are.

Zero-Knowledge Architecture

Good password managers use zero-knowledge encryption. This means your passwords are encrypted on your device, using your master password as the key, before they're uploaded to the server. The company's servers never see your actual passwords — only encrypted data they cannot decrypt.

The Encryption Chain

  1. You enter your master password
  2. The app derives an encryption key from it using PBKDF2 or Argon2 (slow hashing algorithms designed to resist cracking)
  3. This key encrypts your vault using AES-256
  4. The encrypted blob is uploaded to the cloud
  5. Even if the server is breached, the attacker gets encrypted data they cannot open without your master password

What Happens if the Company Gets Hacked?

LastPass was breached in 2022. Attackers got encrypted vault data. Because of zero-knowledge architecture, the data was useless without each user's master password. Users with strong master passwords were not compromised.

The Key Conclusion

A password manager's "one place" is vastly more secure than passwords scattered across sticky notes, spreadsheets, browser saves, and memory. The encryption is the safety net — and it works.
